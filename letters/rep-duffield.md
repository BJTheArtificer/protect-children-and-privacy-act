# Letter to Rep. Matt Duffield

Dear Representative Duffield,

I am writing to ask you to consider co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act, a bill that offers a constitutional, privacy-preserving alternative to California-style operating-system age verification.

The country is moving quickly toward laws that require digital platforms, app stores, operating systems, and devices to verify age. The stated purpose is child protection, and that purpose matters. But the design matters just as much. A poorly designed age-assurance mandate can turn every computer and phone into an identity checkpoint. It can force operating systems to collect, infer, transmit, or retain information about users. It can allow private companies and future government actors to connect identity to lawful adult behavior.

Once that system exists, the original purpose will not be the only purpose. That is how government power grows. A boulder starts rolling downhill with one stated goal, but once it gains momentum, everyone downstream is forced to live with the impact. Arkansas may not be able to stop every state from pushing this technology forward, but we can direct the boulder away from constitutional rights.

This bill is the Arkansas way to do that. It protects children online while requiring that age assurance disclose only the narrowest possible signal: whether the current user is eighteen or older. It prohibits exact-age outputs, date-of-birth disclosure, persistent identifiers, routine callbacks, central logs of lawful adult access, and secondary use of age-assurance data.

The current draft also includes structural safeguards that should be welcome to anyone who takes constitutional liberty seriously:

A five-year sunset clause forces the General Assembly to revisit the program on a regular cycle. If it is working, reauthorization is straightforward. If it is not, the legislature has a built-in opportunity to reform or end it.

Statutory fee caps prevent the program from quietly becoming a revenue source. Any real fee increase requires affirmative legislative action.

Strict liability is limited to the core privacy provisions — selling data, leaking persistent identifiers, lying about the boolean output — rather than every procedural rule. This protects small approved issuers and retailers from being driven out of the program by minor compliance mistakes.

Compelled intermediary remedies — court orders directing ISPs to block sites — are sharply constrained. They are available only against operators who are out of jurisdiction, running sites where more than two-thirds of the material is harmful to minors, who have failed to cure after notice, with clear-and-convincing evidence, and with automatic stay pending appeal. These protections track First Amendment prior-restraint doctrine and are designed to prevent the remedy from being abused.

A coordinated vulnerability disclosure safe harbor protects security researchers who find bugs in the state-certified system. The state cannot threaten researchers who help it improve.

This is not a Second Amendment bill, but it protects the kind of constitutional liberty that Second Amendment supporters understand instinctively. A database does not have to be labeled a gun registry to become one. If a future age-assurance infrastructure can identify lawful adults and connect that status to site visits, app use, purchases, or communities, it can be used to infer who is visiting firearm retailers, training resources, advocacy sites, or gun forums. The same architecture could be used against religious groups, political activists, medical researchers, or any other lawful community.

Arkansas should not accept a model where protecting children requires treating every adult as a suspect. We can do better. We can protect minors while protecting lawful adult privacy, speech, association, and constitutional activity.

One additional feature worth noting, given your focus on responsible governance: the bill is anchored on cost recovery, not revenue generation. Fees are capped at what the program actually costs to run plus a reasonable reserve, with any persistent surplus triggering a mandatory price review. To the extent surplus does accumulate, the bill directs it to Arkansas public schools for computer technology, K-6 STEM curriculum, and classroom supplies, distributed through the Department of Education's existing formulas. This means money raised by protecting Arkansas children online is reinvested in Arkansas children's education. The structure is intentionally discretionary rather than entitlement-based, so school distribution never becomes the program's reason for existence — the privacy architecture remains the point.

Your constitutional-rights record makes you a natural fit for this effort. I hope you will consider helping Arkansas lead with a model that is pro-family, pro-liberty, and anti-surveillance.

Respectfully,
