# Letter to Rep. Denise Garner

Dear Representative Garner,

I am writing to ask you to consider co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. I believe this bill offers a rare opportunity for bipartisan agreement: protecting children online while also protecting the privacy and civil liberties of lawful adults.

The national debate over age verification is moving quickly. California's Assembly Bill 1043, signed in October 2025, will require operating systems and app stores to handle age signals starting in 2027. Federal proposals would push similar requirements nationwide. The stated goal is child protection, and that goal is important. But if the system is designed carelessly, the result could be much larger than child safety. It could turn every computer, phone, and app store into a checkpoint that collects or transmits age identity signals, links users across services, and creates records of lawful adult activity.

That is the concern this bill is designed to solve.

This bill does not weaken Arkansas's ability to protect minors from harmful material. It does not say children should be left unprotected online. Instead, it says the state should require the narrowest workable system: a simple yes-or-no answer about whether the current user is eighteen or older, with strong limits on telemetry, retention, secondary use, persistent identifiers, repeated ID checks, and central logs.

This should matter to people across the political spectrum, and that is exactly why the bill is structured as bipartisan legislation. Conservatives may worry about firearm-related profiling. Progressives may worry about reproductive-health research, LGBTQ resources, immigration-related information, political organizing, labor activity, journalism, or vulnerable communities being tracked. Parents may worry about every device in the home becoming an identity surveillance tool. Ordinary adults may simply believe that lawful reading, browsing, and association should not be logged because of a child-safety law.

The current draft of the bill includes specific civil liberties protections that reflect this bipartisan framing:

The bill explicitly prohibits using age-assurance data for advertising, recommendation, ranking, personalization, pricing, or profiling. This protects users from commercial surveillance dressed up as compliance.

The bill prohibits creating central logs of lawful adult access. No company and no government office is permitted to operate as a central monitor of lawful adult activity.

The bill prohibits persistent identifiers that would let users be tracked across sites, sessions, devices, or accounts.

The bill includes an explicit research and journalism carve-out, so academic study and independent reporting on the program remain possible. Independent oversight is built in.

The bill includes a coordinated vulnerability disclosure safe harbor for security researchers, recognizing that the people who find flaws in government systems are doing public-interest work and should not face liability for it.

The bill includes due process protections for anyone whose credential is wrongly invalidated, with prompt replacement procedures and administrative review.

The bill caps fees in statute, with any real increase requiring affirmative legislative action. The program cannot quietly become a revenue source for the state.

The bill includes a five-year sunset clause forcing the General Assembly to revisit the program on a regular cycle. If it is not working as promised — if the privacy protections turn out to be inadequate, if the program becomes a vector for surveillance, if it imposes unjustified costs — the legislature has a built-in opportunity to fix or end it.

The bill limits any compelled-intermediary remedy to last-resort cases against operators clearly out of reach, with First Amendment-grade procedural protections including clear-and-convincing evidence, automatic stay pending appeal, and expedited appellate review.

That is why this bill is not a left-wing bill or a right-wing bill. It is a civil-liberties bill. It recognizes that privacy is not a luxury. Privacy is part of safety, especially for families, students, patients, advocates, and communities that may be targeted by future misuse of data.

California's approach appears to accept a false choice: protect children or protect privacy. Arkansas does not have to accept that. In Arkansas, we can walk and chew gum. We can protect children online and protect lawful adults from unnecessary surveillance at the same time.

Your background in health, families, education, and public service makes you an important potential voice for this bill. A Democratic co-sponsor would also show that privacy-preserving child protection is not a partisan cause. It is a responsible Arkansas cause.

I would be grateful for the opportunity to discuss this bill with you and hear your thoughts.

Respectfully,

[Your Name]
