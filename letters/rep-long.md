# Letter to Rep. Wayne Long

Dear Representative Long,

I am writing to ask you to consider sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act, a bill designed to protect children online without allowing age-assurance laws to become the foundation for a new surveillance architecture.

Across the country, states are beginning to move toward operating-system-level age verification. California's Assembly Bill 1043, signed into law in October 2025, will require operating systems and app stores to handle age signals starting in 2027. The federal Parents Decide Act and App Store Accountability Act would push similar requirements nationwide. Utah, Texas, Louisiana, and other states have enacted their own variants. That boulder is already rolling downhill. Arkansas may not be able to stop every state or every federal proposal from pushing Big Tech toward age-assurance systems, but Arkansas can decide where the boulder lands. We can either let California-style rules push every computer, phone, and operating system toward identity tracking, or Arkansas can draw a bright constitutional line: protect children, but do not turn lawful adults into data points.

This bill takes the narrowest workable path. It preserves Arkansas's child-protection goals while requiring that any covered age-assurance system disclose only a simple binary result: whether the current user is eighteen or older. No date of birth. No exact age. No reusable identity token. No browsing logs. No issuer callback every time an adult opens a lawful site. No central monitor of lawful adult access.

That is the heart of the Fourth Amendment issue. An age-verification system can be written as a child-safety tool, but if it is designed badly, it becomes a machine for identifying, linking, and profiling lawful adult activity. Once that infrastructure exists, it will not stay limited to one category of content. It can be used to infer political beliefs, religious activity, medical research, advocacy, firearms interest, union organizing, or any other lawful adult behavior someone later decides is suspicious.

The bill in its current form includes provisions specifically designed to prevent that drift. It caps fees in statute at $6 per credential with $1 to the retailer and $5 to the state, with any real increase requiring affirmative legislative action. It includes a five-year sunset clause forcing the General Assembly to reexamine the program on a regular cycle. It narrows strict liability to the core privacy violations rather than every procedural foot-fault. It tightens the definition of harmful-to-minors material to track the *Ginsberg* standard rather than sweep in classic literature. It includes a coordinated vulnerability disclosure safe harbor for security researchers. It limits any compelled-intermediary remedy to sites where more than two-thirds of the material is harmful to minors, with clear-and-convincing evidence and automatic stay pending appeal — protections drawn directly from First Amendment prior-restraint doctrine.

For gun-rights advocates, the danger of poorly designed age-verification is especially obvious. This bill does not regulate firearms and should not be presented as a gun bill. But it does prevent the kind of surveillance rail that could one day be used to identify people who visit firearm retailers, training resources, gun forums, ammunition sites, or Second Amendment advocacy pages. Gun-owner registries do not always begin as gun-owner registries. Sometimes they begin as compliance data.

Arkansas should not copy a California model that says the government can protect children only by making every operating system a checkpoint. In Arkansas, we can walk and chew gum. We can protect minors and protect privacy at the same time.

Your record on Fourth Amendment and constitutional privacy issues makes you uniquely suited to carry this bill. It is pro-family, pro-privacy, anti-surveillance, and conservative in the best sense: it limits power before that power is abused.

I would be grateful for the opportunity to discuss the bill with you and your staff.

Respectfully,

[Your Name]
