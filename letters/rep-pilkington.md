# Letter to Rep. Aaron Pilkington

Dear Representative Pilkington,

I am writing to ask you to consider co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. This bill is about child protection, but it is also about preventing the next generation of digital surveillance from being built in the name of child protection.

The issue is straightforward. Age assurance is coming. California's Assembly Bill 1043, signed in October 2025, will impose age-signal duties on operating systems and app stores starting in 2027. Federal proposals would extend similar requirements nationwide. Once that infrastructure is built, it will not merely answer the question, "Is this person old enough?" It can become a system that links identity, device, account, and online behavior across every interaction.

That is exactly the sort of mission creep Arkansas should reject.

Your work on financial privacy and data-trail concerns makes you an especially important voice on this issue. The same principle applies here as in financial surveillance debates: when government or large corporations create a data trail around lawful activity, they create the conditions for discrimination, blacklisting, profiling, and eventual registration by another name.

This bill is not a gun-rights bill. It does not mention firearms, firearm purchases, or firearm websites. But it protects the same principle gun owners have warned about for years: lawful citizens should not be forced into systems that quietly create lists of who they are, what they buy, what they read, what communities they visit, or what rights they exercise.

The current draft of the bill includes structural protections that should appeal to anyone serious about constraining government and corporate data collection. It strictly prohibits selling, licensing, or making secondary use of age-assurance data. It segregates that data from advertising, recommendation, ranking, and profiling systems. It bans routine issuer callbacks during ordinary use, so no central office can monitor lawful adult activity. It caps statutory fees so the program cannot quietly grow into a revenue machine. It includes a five-year sunset clause forcing the General Assembly to revisit the program. It limits the blacklist to specific invalid credential identifiers — not people — and requires prompt replacement when credentials are compromised through no fault of the holder. It carves out research and journalism from the data restrictions so independent oversight is possible. It includes a coordinated vulnerability disclosure safe harbor for security researchers who find bugs in the state's system.

If an operating system is required to know who is an adult, transmit age signals to apps, and support compliance across the internet, then a bad system could reveal far more than age. It could reveal patterns. It could reveal interests. It could reveal which Arkansans visit gun-related sites, political sites, health sites, religious sites, or advocacy organizations. That is not child safety. That is the foundation of Big Brother.

The Arkansas alternative is better. This bill says: protect children, but limit the system to the narrowest possible output. One binary signal: eighteen-plus or not. No exact age. No birth date. No persistent cross-site identifier. No secondary use. No central adult-access logs. No routine issuer callback. No commercial profiling.

California may believe privacy must be sacrificed to protect children. Arkansas does not have to accept that false choice. We can protect kids and protect adults at the same time.

I should also mention a feature of the bill that fits naturally with your concerns about how government and corporate revenue streams quietly become permanent fixtures. The credential program is structured as a cost-recovery operation, not a revenue source — fees can only cover the actual cost of running the program plus a reasonable reserve. Any persistent surplus triggers a mandatory price review. To the extent surplus does accumulate before prices are adjusted downward, the bill directs it to Arkansas public schools for computer technology, K-6 STEM curriculum, and classroom supplies, coordinated with the Department of Education. The structure is deliberately discretionary rather than entitlement-based, so the program never becomes a hostage to its own surplus. This is the opposite of the budgetary-mission-creep dynamic you've raised concerns about in other contexts.

I hope you will consider joining this effort as a co-sponsor and helping ensure Arkansas sets the national model for privacy-preserving child protection.

Respectfully,

[Your Name]
