# Letter to Rep. Zack Gramlich

Dear Representative Gramlich,

I am writing to ask you to consider co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. This bill advances a simple but important principle: children should be protected online, and their protection should not require creating a surveillance system for everyone else.

As lawmakers across the country consider children's online safety and age-assurance laws, the details matter. A law can be well-intentioned and still produce dangerous infrastructure. If operating systems, devices, app stores, and digital services are pushed into broad age-verification models — as California's Assembly Bill 1043 will require beginning in 2027, and as several federal proposals would extend nationwide — they may begin collecting, transmitting, or retaining age identity data in ways that expose users to tracking, profiling, and misuse.

Arkansas can do better. This bill creates a narrow path that protects minors while sharply limiting what companies and systems may collect or disclose. The only ordinary age-assurance output should be whether the current user is eighteen or older. Not exact age. Not birth date. Not government ID data. Not a reusable identity badge. Not a persistent identifier that follows a user across sites and services.

For children and teenagers, this matters because the internet they inherit should not be built around identity surveillance. For parents, it matters because child protection should not require turning every family device into a tracking device. For adults, it matters because lawful online activity should not be logged, profiled, sold, or linked to identity through a system created in the name of safety.

The current draft addresses children's online privacy concerns directly. The bill prohibits using age-assurance data in advertising, recommendation, ranking, personalization, pricing, or profiling systems — preventing exactly the kind of commercial profiling of young users that has driven so much of the public concern about social media platforms. It prohibits creating central logs of lawful adult access, so the system does not become a record of what individual Arkansans read or watch online. It prohibits routine callbacks to credential issuers during ordinary use, so no central office can monitor lawful adult activity. It limits even the data collected for fraud prevention to what is strictly necessary, with retention periods set by rule and sunset by default.

The bill also recognizes that a law of this scope, building infrastructure that does not currently exist, deserves regular legislative scrutiny. A five-year sunset clause forces the General Assembly to revisit the program on a fixed cycle. The Office of State Technology must report annually to the legislature on program performance. Independent academic and journalism research on the program is explicitly preserved through a research carve-out, so independent oversight remains possible. Security researchers who find vulnerabilities in the state-certified system are protected by a coordinated disclosure safe harbor.

I want to draw your attention to one additional provision that I think matters specifically for someone working on children's online safety and welfare. The credential program is structured as cost-recovery only — it cannot be used as a general revenue source, and any persistent surplus triggers a mandatory price review. To the extent surplus exists in any given year, the bill directs that surplus to Arkansas public schools, distributed through the Department of Education's existing per-pupil distribution formulas, for three specific purposes:

Computer technology for school computer labs and classroom instruction. Science-technology-engineering-and-mathematics curriculum, with explicit statutory emphasis on building foundational computer science and STEM capacity in kindergarten through grade six. And basic classroom supplies — the paper, pencils, and everyday materials teachers too often buy out of pocket.

The structure is deliberately discretionary rather than entitlement-based — meaning the program's primary obligation always remains administering the credential system, and prices must be reduced if surplus consistently exceeds need. The bill is not designed to use children as a permanent revenue justification. But to the extent surplus exists, money raised from a program that protects children online is reinvested in the children whose safety it is built to protect, with particular attention to the foundational K-6 learning years that determine whether children become competent users of technology or become its products.

This is not an anti-child-safety bill. It is the opposite. It is a more trustworthy child-safety bill because it recognizes that safety and privacy must be designed together. A system that families distrust will be evaded. A system that collects too much will be abused. A system that becomes too burdensome will invite resistance from users and businesses alike. But a narrow, binary, privacy-preserving system can meet the child-protection need without creating unnecessary harm — and when it works, it can also help fund the next generation of Arkansas students.

California may move forward with broad mandates and leave privacy as an afterthought. Arkansas can walk and chew gum. We can protect children while protecting civil liberties, consumer privacy, and ordinary families from overcollection and misuse.

Your work on children's online privacy makes your voice especially valuable on this issue. I hope you will consider supporting this bill as a practical, privacy-preserving model for the next generation of online child protection.

Respectfully,
