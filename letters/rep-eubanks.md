# Letter to Rep. Jon Eubanks

Dear Representative Eubanks,

I am writing to ask you to consider co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. This bill addresses one of the most important questions in online safety policy: how do we protect minors without handing Big Tech and future governments the tools to monitor lawful adults?

Other states are pushing toward operating-system, device-level, and app-store-level age assurance. California signed Assembly Bill 1043 into law in October 2025, with effect beginning in 2027. Federal proposals would extend similar duties nationwide. That pressure will force major technology companies to redesign core infrastructure. If Arkansas does nothing, our families may be swept into whatever model California or Washington creates. That model could require operating systems to collect or transmit age-related identity signals, encourage persistent identifiers, and create incentives for tracking users across apps, websites, devices, and accounts.

That is not the only way to protect children. Arkansas can offer a better path.

This bill keeps the child-safety goal but narrows the technology. It says the only lawful external age-assurance output for covered implementations should be a simple binary answer: whether the current user is eighteen or older. It prohibits exact age, date of birth, reusable identity tokens, routine issuer callbacks, central logs of lawful adult access, and secondary use of age-assurance data.

In plain terms: no Big Brother age-verification system. No turning every phone or laptop into a checkpoint. No forcing adults to prove who they are over and over again just to engage in lawful online activity.

The current draft is also designed to be operationally realistic and fiscally sustainable. The credential system uses existing retail gift card distribution rails to reach Arkansans where they already shop. The fee structure — $6 retail, with $1 to the retailer and $5 to the state — is capped in statute, indexed only to inflation, with any real increase requiring affirmative legislative action. The program is designed to be self-funding through credential fees rather than ongoing general appropriation, while still being subject to a five-year sunset clause that forces the General Assembly to reexamine whether it is working.

The bill also includes a thoughtful enforcement architecture. Alleged violations go through a notice-and-cure procedure first, giving operators a fair chance to fix problems before litigation. Bad-faith complaints expose the complainant to liability. Compelled intermediary remedies — court orders directing ISPs to block sites — are available only as a last resort against operators who are out of jurisdiction, running clearly violating sites, with clear-and-convincing evidence and procedural safeguards consistent with First Amendment doctrine. The Office of State Technology must publish technical documentation sufficient for compliance, must operate the system with redundancy and disaster recovery, and must report annually to the legislature on program performance.

This bill should appeal to anyone who cares about online safety. A privacy-preserving system is more likely to be trusted by parents, respected by ordinary users, and defended as a serious alternative to the California model. A broad surveillance-based system will invite backlash, litigation, evasion, and public distrust. A narrow Arkansas model can protect children while avoiding those mistakes.

The boulder is already rolling downhill. Age assurance is coming in some form. Arkansas may not be able to stop every outside mandate, but we can help steer the national conversation. We can say: if age assurance is going to exist, it must be narrow, local, privacy-preserving, and limited to what is necessary.

Your work on online safety issues makes you an important potential co-sponsor. I hope you will consider helping Arkansas become the state that proves child protection and privacy protection can move together.

Respectfully,

[Your Name]
