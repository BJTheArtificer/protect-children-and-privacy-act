# Letter to Rep. Mindy McAlindon

Dear Representative McAlindon,

I am writing to ask you to consider supporting and co-sponsoring the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. This bill builds on Arkansas's commitment to protecting minors from harmful online material, while making sure the tools used to protect children do not create unnecessary risks for families, adults, and ordinary users.

The next phase of online child protection is moving toward age assurance at the device, operating-system, and app-store level. California signed Assembly Bill 1043 into law in October 2025, with effect beginning in 2027. Federal proposals like the Parents Decide Act and the App Store Accountability Act would push similar requirements nationwide. The question is no longer whether age assurance will be debated; the question is what kind of age assurance Arkansas will allow.

California's model points in one direction: broad operating-system mandates, age signals tied to detailed identity information, infrastructure changes, and the possibility of identity-linked compliance systems. Arkansas can point in a better direction. We can say clearly that protecting children does not require building a surveillance machine.

This bill preserves the child-protection purpose of existing Arkansas law while creating a narrow, workable, privacy-preserving compliance path. It allows age assurance to answer the only question that needs to be answered: is the current user eighteen or older? It does not require repeated ID checks at every website. It does not allow exact age, date of birth, government ID images, persistent identifiers, browsing logs, or secondary commercial use of age-assurance data.

This matters for families in concrete ways. Parents want children protected from harmful material, but parents also do not want every device in the home turned into a tracking tool. Adults should not have to trade privacy for child safety. Teenagers should not grow up in a world where the default design of the internet is identity surveillance. Responsible child protection should be narrow, effective, and respectful of constitutional privacy.

The current draft of the bill includes specific provisions that should reassure parents and family advocates:

The bill explicitly addresses Arkansas's family realities. The "knowingly providing a credential to a minor" provision protects parents, stepparents, foster parents, legal guardians, and grandparents or other relatives standing in loco parentis — recognizing that Arkansas has a substantial population of kinship caregivers who would otherwise be exposed to liability under more narrowly drafted legislation.

The credential system is designed around the rhythms of family life. Ordinary adults can get a one-time credential at the point of sale of a new computer, phone, tablet, or console — the same way a gift card is purchased and activated — without registering for any kind of long-term tracking system.

The bill caps fees in statute at $6 per credential, with $1 to the retailer and $5 to the state, so families can plan and the program cannot quietly become expensive over time.

The bill includes a five-year sunset clause forcing the General Assembly to revisit the program. If it is working for Arkansas families, reauthorization will be straightforward. If it is not, the legislature has built-in authority to fix or end it.

The bill includes due process protections so that families wrongly cut off from a credential — through error, theft, fraud, or system failure — get prompt replacement without having to start the proofing process over.

The bill carves out research and journalism so academic study and independent reporting can continue, providing transparency about how the program operates.

There is one more provision in the bill I want to draw to your attention specifically, because I think you will appreciate it: the program is designed so that any revenue beyond what's needed to run it goes directly to Arkansas children. When the credential program runs a surplus, that surplus is distributed to Arkansas public schools and public charter schools through the Department of Education's existing per-pupil distribution formulas, for three specific purposes:

Computer technology for school computer labs and classroom instruction. Science-technology-engineering-and-mathematics curriculum, with statutory emphasis on building foundational computer science and STEM capacity in kindergarten through grade six — recognizing that strong K-6 foundations are essential for later learning in those fields. And basic classroom supplies — paper, writing implements, and the everyday consumable instructional materials that teachers across Arkansas too often pay for out of their own pockets.

In other words: money raised by protecting Arkansas children from harmful online material is reinvested in Arkansas children's education. The bill is deliberately structured so that the program's primary obligation remains administering the credential system, with surplus distribution as a discretionary use of any excess — so the program never becomes a hostage to its own revenue stream and prices can be reduced if surplus persists. But to the extent surplus exists, it goes back to children. That is the kind of provision that, in my view, should appeal to anyone who cares about child welfare in both senses: protecting children from online harm, and equipping them with the tools to learn.

In Arkansas, we can walk and chew gum. We can protect kids and protect privacy at the same time. We can reject the false choice that says every family must either accept harmful online access or accept Big Tech and government identity tracking.

Your work on child protection and your role in Arkansas's existing online harmful-material protections make your voice especially important. This bill does not weaken those protections; it strengthens them by making the compliance model more durable, more privacy-conscious, and more defensible against criticism that age verification inevitably means mass surveillance.

I hope you will consider helping Arkansas lead with a child-protection model that parents can trust and privacy advocates can respect.

Respectfully,
