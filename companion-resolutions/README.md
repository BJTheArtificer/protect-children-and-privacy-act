# Companion Resolutions

This directory contains procedural resolutions intended to be filed and adopted alongside the Arkansas Protects Our Children and Our Privacy Online Act. These resolutions are not part of the bill itself — they are parallel measures adopted by each chamber under its own rulemaking authority, designed to give the substantive scope-limitation in § 4-88-1314(g) of the proposed act independently enforceable procedural teeth within each chamber.

## Files

**`chamber-rules-companion.md`** — Two parallel chamber rules resolutions, one for the House and one for the Senate, that:

- Define what a "covered bill" is (any future bill that would amend, repeal, weaken, or extend the scope limitation in § 4-88-1314)
- Require covered bills to be filed as standalone bills (single-subject rule, cannot be hidden in omnibus bills, appropriations bills, or budget reconciliation)
- Require covered bills to display a plainly worded statement on the first page identifying themselves as scope-limitation modifications
- Require a separate committee hearing with at least 14 days public notice
- Require a published scope-limitation impact statement at least 7 days before the hearing
- Require a recorded roll-call vote on final passage
- Establish a point of order any member can raise against a non-compliant bill, with the Speaker or President ruling
- Permit severance of covered subject matter from non-compliant omnibus bills rather than killing entire unrelated bills
- Require a two-thirds vote to overturn the presiding officer's ruling
- Require a two-thirds vote with three legislative days notice to suspend the rule

## Why these resolutions exist

The substantive scope limitation in § 4-88-1314 — which restricts the program to adult-side determination and prohibits administrative extension to minor-side profiling, monitoring, or behavioral control — is the bill's most important architectural commitment. It is also the provision most vulnerable to silent erosion through future legislation if not procedurally protected.

The bill itself includes procedural expectations in § 4-88-1314(g): single-subject filing, separate committee hearing, recorded vote, public statement of effect, and fiscal-and-privacy impact statement. Those expectations apply by force of statute. But statutory procedural requirements can be ignored by a future legislature, and challenges based on procedural noncompliance are difficult to bring.

The chamber rules companion adds a second, independently enforceable layer. Rules of each chamber are enforced by the chamber itself through points of order. Any member can raise the point of order; the Speaker or President must rule; the ruling stands unless overturned by two-thirds. This means a single legislator who notices that a covered bill is being moved without compliance with the procedural framework can stop it in real time, without waiting for litigation.

The two-thirds threshold for suspension or override is the principal mechanism by which this rule resists erosion. A future chamber retains constitutional authority to amend the rule by simple majority at the start of a session, but cannot circumvent it mid-session by ordinary motion.

## When to file

The chamber rules resolutions should be filed and adopted contemporaneously with passage of the underlying act, so that the procedural protections of § 4-88-1314(g) are operative from the date of enactment. They may also be adopted at the start of any subsequent regular session as part of the standard rules adoption process.

## Important: these are not statutory provisions

Each chamber's rules are adopted by resolution, not by enacted statute. They take effect on adoption and are enforced internally by the chamber's parliamentarian and presiding officer, not by courts. They do not require gubernatorial signature.

The procedural requirements of § 4-88-1314(g) of the underlying act apply by force of statute regardless of whether these chamber rules are adopted. The chamber rules add a second enforcement mechanism; they do not replace the statutory framework.

## Constitutional limits

Neither resolution claims to bind a future chamber against its constitutional authority to amend its own rules. The findings in subsection (h)(3) of each rule explicitly acknowledge this. The practical protection comes from the political and procedural cost of amending the rule, not from any claim of permanence. This acknowledgment is included for the same reason as the parallel acknowledgment in § 4-88-1314(g)(8) of the underlying act: to ensure that the rule itself is not struck as an unconstitutional entrenchment, which would leave nothing in place.

## For other states

If you are adapting the bill for use in another state, consider drafting parallel chamber rules for your state's House and Senate. The same logic applies: the substantive privacy architecture is most durable when it is both statutorily protected and procedurally protected. The text in this companion can serve as a model, adapted to the specific naming conventions, presiding-officer titles, and rulemaking procedures of your state's chambers.
