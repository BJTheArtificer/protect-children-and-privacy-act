# Research

This directory contains the source legal authorities the bill is built on. These are public-domain government documents — federal statutes, Supreme Court opinions, Congressional Research Service overviews, and Arkansas legislation — included in the repository so reviewers, journalists, and adapters in other states can examine the legal foundation directly rather than tracking it down themselves.

All documents here were obtained from official government sources (supremecourt.gov, congress.gov, and Arkansas legislative resources). They are works of the United States or of the State of Arkansas, public domain, and may be redistributed without restriction.

## Files

### `47 U.S. Code § 230 - Protection for private blocking and screening of offensive material.md`

The full statutory text of Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, the federal statute that limits state authority to impose liability on internet service providers, search engines, and similar intermediaries for third-party content. The bill is drafted to operate within the limits Section 230 imposes, and several provisions — particularly the federal-law savings and severability clause in Section 9, the limited intermediary remedy in § 4-88-1313(f)(2), and the construction rules throughout — exist to ensure compatibility with this federal preemption framework.

Source: [47 U.S.C. § 230](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/47/230)

### `S230-overview-R46751.8.pdf`

Congressional Research Service overview of Section 230, providing background on the statute's history, scope, judicial interpretation, and the limits it places on state and federal regulation of online intermediaries. CRS reports are prepared for Congress and made publicly available through crsreports.congress.gov. They are non-partisan analytical documents and are uncopyrighted.

This report informs the bill's approach to intermediary remedies and the careful drafting of § 4-88-1313 to avoid Section 230 preemption while still providing meaningful enforcement against operators clearly violating Arkansas law.

Source: [crsreports.congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R46751#_Toc155275791).

### `FSCvPaxton-23-1122_3e04.pdf`

The 2025 Supreme Court opinion in *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton*, the case upholding Texas H.B. 1181's age-verification requirements for commercial pornography websites under intermediate scrutiny. This is the most directly relevant Supreme Court precedent for the bill, and the bill's core "substantial portion" threshold (more than one-third of material harmful to minors) is drawn from the Texas statute upheld in this case.

The bill also draws several procedural protections — particularly the clear-and-convincing-evidence standard in § 4-88-1313(f)(2)(B) and the automatic stay pending appeal in § 4-88-1313(f)(2)(F) — from First Amendment prior-restraint doctrine that *Paxton* operates within rather than overrules.

Source: [supremecourt.gov](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/23-1122_3e04.pdf).

### `PorkvRoss-21-468_5if6.pdf`

The 2023 Supreme Court opinion in *National Pork Producers Council v. Ross*, the dormant Commerce Clause case that gave states substantially more room to regulate goods and services that cross state lines. This case is what makes the bill's uniform-application safe harbor (§ 4-88-1304(g)(3)) and the explicit non-extraterritoriality clauses (§ 4-88-1304(g)(7) and (g)(8)) constitutionally durable. Without *Pork Producers*, the bill would face a much harder dormant Commerce Clause challenge.

Source: [supremecourt.gov](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/22pdf/21-468_5if6.pdf).

### `AR-ACT612.pdf`

Arkansas Act 612 of 2023, the Protection of Minors from Distribution of Harmful Material Act — the existing Arkansas law that this bill amends. Reading this is essential for understanding what the bill changes versus what it preserves. The bill does not replace Arkansas's existing child-protection framework; it adds a privacy-preserving compliance path for covered implementations alongside it.

Source: [Arkansas legislative resources](https://www.arkleg.state.ar.us/Home/FTPDocument?path=%2FACTS%2F2023R%2FPublic%2FACT612.pdf).

## Why these specific documents

The legal architecture of the bill rests on five pillars, and each document above corresponds to one of them:

1. **Federal preemption boundary** — Section 230 (statute and CRS overview) defines what states cannot do.
2. **First Amendment compatibility** — *Paxton* defines what age-verification regimes survive intermediate scrutiny.
3. **Interstate commerce compatibility** — *Pork Producers* defines what state regulation of multistate technology systems can require.
4. **Existing state framework** — Act 612 defines what Arkansas already protects, and what the new bill amends versus preserves.

Anyone who wants to challenge, defend, adapt, or improve this bill should be working from these sources directly. They are included here so that work can begin immediately.

## What's not here

Cases and statutes that influenced the drafting but did not need to be reproduced in full include *Miller v. California* (1973) (the obscenity test), *Ginsberg v. New York* (1968) (the variable-obscenity standard for minors), *Bantam Books v. Sullivan* (1963) and *Freedman v. Maryland* (1965) (prior-restraint procedural protections), *Reno v. ACLU* (1997) and *Ashcroft v. ACLU* (2004) (earlier internet-regulation cases), and *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) (the metadata-aggregation Fourth Amendment case). These are widely available from any Supreme Court database and from the original opinions on supremecourt.gov.

State-level age-verification statutes from Texas (H.B. 1181), Utah, Louisiana, and California (A.B. 1043) are also relevant context. They are available from each state's legislative resources and have been included in the analysis but are not reproduced here.

Federal proposals including the Parents Decide Act and the App Store Accountability Act are tracked at congress.gov.
